Shooting for the Edit 

Shooting for the Edit, it's like making a cake, if you have more ingredients you have more options.
It's used by experienced cameraman, self shooters, and video journalists. Not only is a way to ensures the material you get will cut in the edit to help you tell the story, but also helps you gather options for the edit.
For self shooters and video journalists, merging the technical and editorial,  through these elements of screen grammar, it helps you focus on the content with emphasis the story.

 

1. What's the Story?

Ask your self, what am I trying to tell the viewer? what is the the overall objective?
What part of the story is this sequence/shoot going to help me tell?

2. What's the Action?

What are the activities or events? What is going to happen?
That is different from the story, in the sense that the action illustrates story points, for instants "having an argument" vs "walking in".

if the story is that they are having an argument, and you know the action is that someone is walking in, then this fill influence which shoot you want, to best tell it.

if you are "in medias res" follow where your eyes go, what grabs your attention, ie someone chopping carrots, your eyes go on the carrots, and therefore a close up of the carrots being chopped.

Think:
Who? What? Where? When?
------------ 
[ Why? and how? if they stand out]


take control, walk a round the place/room, think, take your time (if you can) let your brain process the info.

tip to move people: "you would look better if" you move overthere
if possible rehearsal will help getting it right.

dealing with the contributors tell them what you are going to do before you are going to do it, and reassure them. 


Example, world champion of texting, 
what's the story? relationship with phone
Action? picking up phone and texting



3. What are the shoots?

Think of sequences with beginning middle and end

minimum 4 shoots 

close up of hands
1 - hands and the object - close up

doing the action,ie picking up,  excluding everything else

 ie close up of phone being picked up and put down (thinking - possible begining and end)

ie Repeating the all action,  
close up of object









2  - two shoot of contributor and object - Wide or Medium
two shoot
Doing the all of action, and possibly walking in and out of frame.

ie picking up phone, texting, putting down, walking away.


two shoot











3 - contributor's face - Showing eyes, generally, Lowe angle, close up.
doing the action, excluding everything else

 ie Lowe angle, put camera on eye line [rule of third?]

 4- Master Shoot
wide shoot, that establishes the scene generally from farther away.


Other Shoots to consider


4 - Over the shoulder shoot - medium or wider
Repeat the action and film from for the shoulder.
It can be someone walking, doing an action, looking at something.

 








5.  POV
if you don't frame the shoulder this can easily become a POV shoot.

5 - Extra shoots
where you can see only part of the action, (good for continuity cut)
ie someone chopping carrots, if you frame it from under the table so that you see the the face, and arms chopping, but not the knife and the carrot, you can then use it to cut action maintaining continuity.

6 - "Art Shoot"
wide, medium, macro.
Wants to be the best shoot option to capture attention and start with in the edit,
by putting the camera somewhere, where you normally wouldn't put your eyes.

ie macro shoot, phone in the front, foreground with contributor out of focus.

7 - Piece to camera in medias res -Optional

Sometimes it might be suited, that if you are having them doing an action over and over again, for changing angle and shoot size, at the end when they are doing it for the last time, when they are in the crucial part of the action you could ask them for a short comment on it,(ie how does it feel? or what is it like to?) it might make a good sound bite to bridge the interview with the actuality sequence.

8- Cutawayas/GVs
is a shoot that "cuts away and brings you back".  
When non-repeatable actuality/action it's finished or before and film details of location.
ie outside shoot of building.
Cutaways, function is that bring you out and then back in again.

 Shoots

- Hold shoots for 10sec min 
- Overlap the action, it gives options to disguise edit.
 -Change shoot size and angle you are filming from, to ensure it will cut together.
[10 sec ->move-> 10 sec->move->...]
- Make close up close, keeping wide lens, and camera lens close to action/object/person.
"TV is a close up medium" but don't forget your establishing.

- When filming contributor's face(close up medium or wide put camera on eye line, and try to have light on the side of the eyeline?)


- Don't cross the line, to avoid confusing viewers. 

- and establish the geography of the place.


Sound

- Monitor with headphones

On board shotgun mic

- use shock mount
- Keep mic close enough, 
for instance you can do this keeping camera zoomed out on wide lens, and shooting close ups up close. that way also mic is closer to sound source (ie someone chopping carrots).

Shooting actuality and/or non repeatable actions

the considerations above are all very well, if you can get your contributors to repeat the action a bunch of times, however if it's a more hectic situation, or non-repeatable for whatever reason, then it is likely to follow in these two scenario.

one where the situation is completely outside of your control,
and other one where you got a bit more control(in terms of having the action repeated) but there's a non reversible point in the action.

The principles we use are the same, but let's have a look at how we make the most them.

1. if no control on actuality unfolding

"Fly on the wall". 
Shooting actuality(and non-repeatable actions) makes you better and more organised, as you have to be faster in planning and executing.


Ask yourself:
- What's the story? Porpouse?
- What's the action?  What shoots do you need?
ie behind, cover action
- plan and organise, as much as possible, 
for instance if it's a discussion, have them sit all at same side of the table, if possible.
- Cutaways: Using cutaways is a good way to cover actuality, getting these done, before or after the "non-repeatable action".
  

2. If partial control on actuality

ie for instance something where you can ask the contributor to repeat part of the action up to a "non-return point"
ie a chef cracking an egg as part of a cooking demonstration.
Let's assume they only have one egg.

  Have as much control as possible by the circumstances:
- repeat action with master shoot-wide, Two shoot-medium/wide, close up of object, up to non reverse point.
- repeat with focus(close up) on non-return point, ie cracking egg
- pretend repeat with only face - close up.
- over the shoulder






For interviews

filming the interview

for formal interviews with one camera,  Order of questions going from  general (context/facts) to specific(emotional).

and change shoot size from wider to medium, close up as questions narrow down to the emotional.

Goal of interview is to making people comfortable to get the emotions, based on what's the story.

Framing

use rule of third 
- eyes on top third line of the frame
- eyes looking in space torwards interviewer
- main light source on the side of the headroom
- Back of head away from the frame but not too much dead space
- Little bit of space above the head but not to much
 


when adjusting frame, zooming out or closing in, as long as you keep eyes on top line of the thirds composition will look ok.


The light, weather lighting the interview, or using natural light, should be on looking room side.
and you should, if possible, see rereflection of light in their eyes.

you can use your hands to check how is the light in a particular place[||--||]

check the edge of the frame, if there are leading lines, that give perspective, try and make them enter the frame in the corners.

Sound

Sound for formal/sit down interviews, use lapel/wireles mic when posisble, and/or mic stand with shotgun mic, best is to use both. 
As lapel less sensible to background noise but not as good sound quality as shotgun mic, which has however a less localised pattern.


Sequence

Together with the interview, you shoot the sequence that helps you tell that story.
ie shoots of what they are talking about, or then doing something related to the story.

[ie shooting someone feading ginnie pig]


Shoot List

Shoot List, it's a useful tool to prep for a shoot, and ensure at the very list the minimum requirements for the edit. it is likely thou that if things they don't go as expected it will change. But identifying as much as possible of the Story, Action, and Shoots before hand, makes you as prepare as you can be.
